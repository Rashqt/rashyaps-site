The beginning — a desk and a dream

I didn't have a plan. I had a laptop, a bad internet connection, and a genuinely embarrassing number of YouTube tabs open. I was trying to figure out what I wanted to make — not what I should make. What I wanted to make.

There's a difference most people skip past. "Should" is about external validation. "Want" is about internal pull. I spent a long time in "should" before I found my way back to "want."

This space is the result of following the pull. It's messier and quieter than most content out there — and that's exactly why it feels like something worth staying in.

Learning to code — out of frustration

I didn't start learning web development because I wanted to be a developer. I started because I had ideas I couldn't execute. I'd look at websites that felt wrong and think: I could do that better. But I couldn't — yet.

So I opened a YouTube tutorial at 11 PM. Then another. Then another. By 3 AM I had a terrible-looking webpage that was entirely mine. That feeling — of making something that didn't exist before — is the reason I kept going.

HTML to CSS to JavaScript to React to backend things I still find a little terrifying. But the underlying motivation was always the same: build what you imagine, not just imagine it.

Creating publicly — terrifying, necessary

The first thing I put out publicly was bad. Not catastrophically bad — just clearly the work of someone who hadn't done it before. I almost deleted it the morning after.

I didn't. And that was one of the better decisions I've made. Because leaving it up meant I had to live with it, learn from it, and do better next time.

That's the uncomfortable truth about learning in public: it exposes you before you're ready. But waiting until you're ready has a catch. You never feel ready. You just eventually run out of reasons not to start.

Where I am now — building slowly

Right now, I'm somewhere in the middle. Not at the beginning, not at a destination. Just in the work — building things that interest me, writing when I have something worth saying, sharing with a small community who seem to get it.

The newsletter has 600+ readers. The blog has posts that have made strangers feel less alone at 2 AM. The projects are small but real. And I'm slowly getting better at the thing that matters most: making work that actually lands.

If you're reading this, you're part of the reason it's worth continuing. Thank you for being here.
