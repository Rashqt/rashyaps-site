About me

Hey. I'm Rash.
I make things that matter.

I'm a Gen Z creator, developer, and writer who spends too many nights making things that don't fit into any one category — and has stopped trying to make them fit.

This site is my corner of the internet. Not curated for an algorithm. Not optimized for impressions. Just the closest thing I have to a creative home — where my blogs, projects, and late-night thoughts live together in one place.

The beginning — a desk and a dream

I didn't have a plan. I had a laptop, a bad internet connection, and a genuinely embarrassing number of YouTube tabs open. I was trying to figure out what I wanted to make — not what I should make. What I wanted to make.

There's a difference most people skip past. "Should" is about external validation. "Want" is about internal pull. I spent a long time in "should" before I found my way back to "want."

This space is the result of following the pull. It's messier and quieter than most content out there. I think that's why it resonates with certain people — the ones who are also still figuring things out and would rather do that honestly than pretend they've already arrived.

Learning to code — out of frustration

I didn't start learning web development because I wanted to be a developer. I started because I had ideas I couldn't execute. I'd look at websites that felt wrong and think: I could do that better. But I couldn't — yet.

So I opened a YouTube tutorial at 11 PM. Then another. Then another. By 3 AM I had a terrible-looking webpage that was entirely mine. That feeling — of making something that didn't exist before — is the reason I kept going.

The technical skills followed. HTML to CSS to JavaScript to React to backend things I still find a little terrifying. But the underlying motivation was always the same: build what you imagine, not just imagine it.

Creating publicly — terrifying, necessary

The first thing I put out publicly was bad. Not catastrophically bad — just clearly the work of someone who hadn't done it before. I almost deleted it the morning after I posted it.

I didn't. And that was one of the better decisions I've made. Not because the work was secretly great — it wasn't. But because leaving it up meant I had to live with it, learn from it, and do better next time.

That's the uncomfortable truth about learning in public: it exposes you before you're ready. But waiting until you're ready has a catch. You never feel ready. You just eventually run out of reasons not to start.

So I started. I kept going. And gradually, without noticing exactly when it happened, I became someone who actually knew how to make things people wanted to read and use.

Where I am now — building slowly

Right now, I'm somewhere in the middle. Not at the beginning, not at some destination. Just in the work — building things that interest me, writing when I have something worth saying, sharing with a small community of people who seem to get it.

The newsletter has 600+ readers. The blog has posts that have made strangers feel less alone at 2 AM. The projects are small but real. And I'm slowly getting better at the thing that matters most to me: making work that actually lands.

If you're reading this, you're part of the reason it's worth continuing. Thank you for being here.

Values

What I actually believe

The things I keep coming back to, even when they're inconvenient.

01

Honesty over polish

A rough truth is more valuable than a smooth performance. I'd rather say something real than something impressive.

02

Quality over consistency

I'd rather post once when something is ready than ten times to fill a schedule. Rhythm matters more than frequency.

03

Small but real

A hundred people who genuinely connect with your work is worth more than ten thousand passive impressions.

04

Human-first design

Everything I build should feel like it was made by a person who cares, not a system that optimizes.

05

Quiet ambition

I don't need to broadcast the work before it's done. The building matters more than the announcement.

06

Always learning

Growth shared is growth compounded — for everyone involved. I'd rather be visibly learning than invisibly stagnant.
